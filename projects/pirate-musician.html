Overview
Background

This is my design research project, sponsored by the the ExCITe Center, Drexel University's in-house design innovation lab. I worked alongside the director, Youngmoo Kim, who is a pioneer in music technology research. I was responsible for conducting user research, prototyping, and usability testing.

Music notation software is not exactly known for being intuitive. Most music notation software follows a point-and-click paradigm, where the user clicks on note duration and then clicks on the staff to plot the note, which can be difficult to do precisely.

The Problem

Within musical circles, it is just accepted that music notation software is subpar. The music community needs notation software that is intuitive and empowers them to be creative without any usability obstacles. The problem space for music notation software is enormous, so the goal of this research project would be to narrow down on a specific usability problem.

“Sibelius [a commonly used music notation app] is truly revolutionary in the sense that no matter how carefully you try to work with it, it always seems to be trying to rise up against you.”
User Interviews

To better understand the problem space, I used a conducted interviews, heuristic analysis, usability tests. The goal of the interviews is to understand the world that the user lives in, while the goal of usability tests is to narrow in on specific usability problems.

My protocol included questions like:

  1. What role does technology play in your musical studies?
  2. When was the last time you used [music notation program]? Could you tell me about your experience?
  3. Could you tell me about any particularly frustrating times when you used [music notation program]?
  4. Is there a specific type of music notation that you had difficulty notating in [music notation program]?
Interview Insights

Here is a summary of some interview insights:

  1. One user tried using Sibelius, but gave up out of frustration after a short while and resorted to pencil and paper.
  2. When writing more complex music, the music notation program would be overly pedantic when the user wants more freedom in how they write.
  3. Users were overwhelmed by the number of keyboard shortcuts that are available in music notation software. They feel that with practice, they could become proficient, but the learning curve is quite steep.
"They [the music notation app] did have a long, a huge index of [keyboard] shortcuts, but it's way too cumbersome to look for something and then scroll in the index to try to find the shortcut when I could have just clicked on it."
Heuristic Analysis

Together with my advisor Youngmoo Kim, we conducted heuristic analysis based on Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics. Heuristic analyses are useful for finding usability problems in a user interface without the need for users. We can also be much more thorough with the analysis than with a usability test, as there are no time limits or other restrictions. That being said, I did conduct usability testing later to find any usability problems that I might've missed during the heuristic analysis.

Mode Errors - Violation of Nielsen's First Heuristic

While conducting the heuristic analysis, the most egregious violation I discovered was with Nielsen's first heuristic: Visibility of System Status.

Usability Testing

While there is much to learn from conducting user interviews and heuristic analysis, these two methods don't show how users actually interact with the software. As Jakob Nielsen stated, "pay attention to what users do, not what they say." This is why I conducted usability tests.
