Creative Achievement Questionnaire (CAQ)

Role
UI/UX Designer
Overview
This project is based on a paper by Shelley Carson, PhD and Jordan Peterson, PhD. During their research, they came up with a self-report questionnaire that attempts to quantify an individual’s creative achievement. The questionnaire covers achievement across 10 domains of creativity (e.g. visual arts, music, dance, etc.) For the original paper, please click here
Pain Points
While filling out the survey, I found it to be cumbersome. It required jumping around between sections, manually calculating your score, and overall was a tedious experience. I started questioning whether it was worth the trouble to get my CAQ score. The research was certainly thorough, but the experience of filling out the questionnaire felt drawn-out. I wanted to make the experience and accessible.
Goals
  1. Reduce the amount of time it takes to fill out the survey.
  2. Designed for people of all ages
  3. Create a fun and interactive exprience
Original Flow
Carson’s research explores achievement in 10 domains of creativity. The original questionnaire is divided into 10 sections accordingly. In the beginning of each section, the user is first if they have any training or recognize talent. If they do, they are instructed to fill out the rest of that section. If not, they are instructed to go to the next area. Majority (if not at all) users won’t fill out every section. The below diagram illustrates the inefficiency of the original form.
New Flow
To reduce the amount of time the user spent filling out the form. Instead of having the user go through all 10 domains, the user is asked upfront which of the domains they have training or recognized training. Please see diagram below.

Visual Design
For the visual design, I used Adobe XD and went for a bubbly feel, using bright colors and round text. This is to turn the dry, pencil and paper experience into something more fun and interactive.
