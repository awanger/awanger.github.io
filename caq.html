Creative Achievement Questionnaire

Role
UI/UX Designer
Overview
This project is based on a paper by Shelley Carson, PhD and Jordan Peterson, PhD. During their research, they came up with a self-report questionnaire that attempts to quantify an individual’s creative achievement. The questionnaire covers achievement across 10 domains of creativity (e.g. visual arts, music, dance, etc.) For the original paper, please click here
Pain Points
While filling out the survey, I found it to be cumbersome. It required jumping around between sections, manually calculating your score, and overall was a tedious experience. I started questioning whether it was worth the trouble to get my CAQ score. The research was certainly thorough, but the experience of filling out the questionnaire felt drawn-out. I wanted to make the experience and accessible.
Goals
  1. Reduce the amount of time it takes to fill out the survey.
  2. Designed for people of all ages
  3. Create a fun and interactive exprience
Screenshot of the original questionnaire. Please click here to see the full PDF
Original Flow
Carson’s research explores achievement in 10 domains of creativity. The original questionnaire is divided into 10 sections accordingly. In the beginning of each section, the user is first if they have any training or recognize talent. If they do, they are instructed to fill out the rest of that section. If not, they are instructed to go to the next category. Majority (if not at all) users won’t fill out every section. The below diagram illustrates the inefficiency of the original form.
New Flow
To reduce the amount of time the user spent filling out the form. Instead of having the user go through all 10 domains, the user is asked upfront which of the domains they have experience in. There are then only asked questions pertaining to the domains they selected.
Visual Design
For the visual design, I used Adobe XD and went for a bubbly feel, using bright colors and round text. I wanted to turn the pencil and paper experience into something more fun and interactive.
Linguistic Detail
The paper-and-pencil questionnaire felt like another long form to fill out, like filing your taxes. The original form basically commands the user to fill in certain criteria. To make it less dreadful, I rephrased all of the original statements into questions. So it feels more like a conversation between the user and the system.

For example instead of:
“Place a check mark beside sentences that apply to you”
We replace it with the question:
“Which of these statements apply to you?”
Early Prototype
The initial prototype had a little helper character that I ended up scrapping because it took too much screen space. I also had to redo the whole interface as it was lacking visual hierarchy.
