Creative Achievement Questionnaire

Role
UI/UX Designer
Overview
This project was inspired by a research paper by Shelley Carson, Ph.D advised by Jordan Peterson. During their research, they came up with a self-report questionnaire that attempts to quantify an individual’s creative achievement. The questionnaire covers achievement across ten domains of creativity (e.g. visual arts, music, dance, etc.) For the original paper, please click here
Pain Points
While filling out the survey, I found it to be cumbersome. It required jumping around between sections, manually calculating your score, and overall was a tedious experience. I started questioning whether it was worth the trouble to get my CAQ score. The research was certainly thorough, but the experience of filling out the questionnaire felt drawn-out. I wanted to make the experience and accessible.
Goals
  1. Reduce the amount of time it takes to fill out the survey.
  2. Designed for people of all ages
  3. Create a fun and interactive exprience
Original Flow
Carson’s research explores achievement in ten creative domains. The original questionnaire is divided into ten sections accordingly. At the beginning of each section, the user is first asked if they have any training or recognize talent for that domain. If so, they are instructed to fill out the rest of that section. If not, they are instructed to go to the next domain. Majority (if not at all) users won’t need to fill out every section. The below diagram illustrates the inefficiency of the original form.
Screenshot of the original questionnaire. Notice how the user is shown every section and instructed to skip the ones that don't apply to them.
New Flow
To reduce the amount of time the user spent filling out the form. Instead of having the user go through all ten domains, the user is asked upfront which of the domains they have experience in. They are only asked questions pertaining to the domains they selected; essentially the irrelevant sections are hidden from them.
Visual Design
For the visual design, I used Adobe XD and went for a bubbly feel, using bright colors and round text. I wanted to turn the pencil and paper experience into something more fun and interactive.
Linguistic Detail
The paper-and-pencil questionnaire felt like another long form to fill out, like filing your taxes. The original form basically commands the user to fill in certain criteria. To make it less dreadful, I rephrased all of the original statements into questions.

For example instead of:
“Place a check mark beside sentences that apply to you”
We replace it with the question:
“Which of these statements apply to you?”
This way feels more like a conversation between the user and the system.
Early Prototype
The initial prototype had a little helper character that I ended up scrapping because it took too much screen space. I also had to redo the whole interface as it was lacking visual hierarchy.
